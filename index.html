Defensive sectors are supposed to protect you when markets fall. But a demand shock and an inflation shock are not the same crisis — and this study finds that sector "defensiveness" flips depending on which one you're in.

The Question

Does "defensive sector" mean the same thing every time markets crash?

During stock market downturns, which sectors experience the smallest percent decline — and does that hierarchy shift depending on what actually caused the crash?

Two structurally different crashes were compared using three metrics, computed sector-by-sector from adjusted closing prices across Technology, Healthcare, Energy, Financial, Consumer Staples and Defense.

Metric 01

Maximum Drawdown

The largest peak-to-trough percentage loss during the crash window — how much capital an investor actually lost at the worst point.

Metric 02

Crash Beta

Sensitivity relative to the S&P 500 during the crash itself. Above 1.0 means a sector fell harder than the market; below 1.0 means it cushioned the blow.

Metric 03

Recovery Time

Trading days needed for a sector's price to climb back to its pre-crash high — how long the pain actually lasted.

Covid-19 Crash

An external panic

A sudden, exogenous halt to economic activity. Liquidity dried up overnight, but once policy responded, it resolved fast.

2022 Bear Market

A slow structural squeeze

Aggressive rate hikes to fight inflation put sustained pressure on valuations and lending margins over many months.

The Central Finding

Same sector. Opposite crash. Opposite outcome.

Plotting each sector's maximum drawdown in both crashes side by side reveals crossing lines — sectors don't hold a fixed rank. Energy and Defense were the worst-hit sectors in the panic-driven crash, then became two of the strongest performers in the inflation-driven one. Technology did the exact reverse.

ENERGY

Worst drawdown of any sector in Covid (−50.9%, 596-day recovery) — then the second-smallest drawdown of any sector in the bear market.

TECHNOLOGY

Fastest recovery of any sector after Covid (87 days) — then the deepest drawdown and highest crash beta (1.53) once rates rose.

Hypothesis vs. Evidence

What the data confirmed — and what it didn't expect

Healthcare and Consumer Staples will protect capital in any downturn — but Energy will rank worst in a demand-driven crash and best in an inflation-driven one.

Confirmed

Healthcare is the only true all-weather defensive sector

Lowest beta, smallest drawdowns, and the fastest recovery of any sector in both crashes. No other sector matched this consistency.

Partially confirmed

Consumer Staples protects on the way down, not on the way up

Smallest drawdown of any sector in both crashes — but one of the slowest recoveries. Capital preservation and recovery speed turned out to be two separate skills entirely.

Confirmed

Energy fully reversed between crashes

Worst sector in the demand shock (−50.9%, 596 days), second-best in the inflation shock (−33.7%, 350 days). The clearest evidence that crash catalyst — not sector label — drives outcomes.

Unexpected

Defense mirrored Energy in reverse

Not part of the original hypothesis. Above-market beta and the single slowest recovery in Covid (733 days) — then the fastest recovery of any sector in the bear market (186 days).

Sector defensiveness is not a fixed property — the macroeconomic catalyst of a crash decides which sectors protect capital, and which amplify losses.

About this study

47 stocks across six sectors, benchmarked against the S&P 500 (SPY), analyzed across the Covid-19 crash (Feb–Mar 2020) and the 2022 bear market (Jan–Oct 2022) using adjusted closing price data.

Data & tools

Historical prices sourced from Yahoo Finance via the tidyquant package in R. Metrics computed independently for each crash window.
